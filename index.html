🔍 Why Students Score Below Band 6 on Task 2

The Four Band 5.5 Errors
  1. Writing a list of ideas instead of a developed argument
  2. Using general vocabulary: good, bad, important, many people
  3. Never fully developing one point — always moving on too quickly
  4. Confusing "discuss both views" with "give your opinion"

📐 The Band 7 Essay Structure

¶ 1 — Introduction (3–4 sentences) → Paraphrase the question (do NOT copy it) → State your position clearly → Preview your two main arguments ¶ 2 — Body 1 (5–6 sentences) → Topic sentence: one clear, specific claim → Explain WHY this is true (mechanism / reason) → Give a specific example (real or plausible) → Link back to the question ¶ 3 — Body 2 (5–6 sentences) → Same structure as Body 1 ¶ 4 — Conclusion (2–3 sentences) → Restate your position in new words → Summarise the two main reasons → Optional: wider implication
Processing Instruction Task — Do this BEFORE writing

Read these two sentences. Which sounds more academic? Why?

(A) I think governments should spend money on public transport.
(B) It is my contention that governments have a responsibility to prioritise public transport investment.

Notice: passive construction "it is my contention that" · nominalisation "investment" · modal of obligation "have a responsibility"

Day 2 · Tuesday

Listening — Sections 3 & 4

Section 4 is an academic monologue — the hardest listening section. Most Band 5.5 errors here come from poor prediction and spelling, not from inability to understand English.

ListeningSections 3 & 4Prediction techniqueSpelling traps

❌ Why Band 5.5 Students Miss Answers

Four Core Errors
  • They panic when they miss one answer and lose the next two
  • They do not predict what type of answer to listen for
  • They mishear homophones: their/there, two/too/to, write/right
  • They do not use the 30-second preparation time effectively

🔮 The Prediction Technique — Band 7 Habit

Before the audio plays, read each question and ask three questions:

  • What grammatical form is needed? (noun? number? name? verb?)
  • What category of word? (place? time? percentage? adjective?)
  • What synonyms might the speaker use for the keywords in the question?
Question: "The library closes at _____ on Fridays."
✓ PREDICTION
A time (e.g., 6 pm / half past five). Listen for time phrases. The speaker may say "shuts" instead of "closes."

📝 Section 4 Note-Taking Method

Section 4 is an academic monologue. Use this structure while listening:

Main topic: _________________________________ Sub-topic 1: _________________________________ → Key detail: _____________________________ → Example: _____________________________ Sub-topic 2: _________________________________ → Key detail: _____________________________ → Key detail: _____________________________

Your answers live in the detail lines. The sub-topic lines give you context.

Day 3 · Wednesday

Reading — TFNG & Matching Headings

The two question types most responsible for keeping students at Band 5.5. The TFNG distinction and the Matching Headings strategy are learnable in a single focused session.

ReadingTrue/False/Not GivenMatching HeadingsTime management

⚠️ True / False / Not Given — The Most Misunderstood Question Type

TRUE = The text clearly says THE SAME THING as the statement FALSE = The text clearly CONTRADICTS the statement NOT GIVEN = The statement is NOT MENTIONED AT ALL There is no evidence either way
Critical Rule

NOT GIVEN does not mean "I couldn't find it." It means the text provides no information to confirm OR deny the statement. If you searched the text and found nothing related to the topic of the statement — that is NOT GIVEN.

The FALSE Trap

Students mark things FALSE when the text just doesn't discuss it. If the statement is about "the cost of the project" and the text says nothing about cost — that is NOT GIVEN, not FALSE. FALSE requires a direct contradiction.

✗ WRONG APPROACH
Student reads: "The text doesn't say this so it must be false." → Marks FALSE
✓ CORRECT APPROACH
"The text doesn't discuss this topic at all. There is no information to confirm or deny it." → Marks NOT GIVEN

4-Step TFNG Method:

  1. Read the statement carefully
  2. Find the relevant section (use keywords to locate it)
  3. Compare the statement to the text word by word
  4. Ask: does the text CONFIRM, CONTRADICT, or IGNORE this statement?

📑 Matching Headings — Band 7 Strategy

Why students get this wrong
  • They read the whole paragraph looking for matching words — this is slow and misleading
  • They match words rather than matching function / main idea

The 3-Step Method:

  1. Read the first sentence — usually contains the main idea
  2. Read the last sentence — often signals what the paragraph is about
  3. Ignore details in the middle — they support the heading, they do not define it
✗ WRONG MATCH
Paragraph begins: "Despite initial scepticism, several governments adopted the policy." → Student picks "Government policy"
✓ CORRECT MATCH
The function is about acceptance of something controversial → "Growing acceptance of a controversial approach"

The 90-Second Rule

If a question takes more than 90 seconds, mark it and move on. Return at the end. A question you cannot answer in 90 seconds is unlikely to be answered in 3 minutes either.

Day 4 · Thursday

Speaking — Parts 1, 2 & 3

The speaking exam rewards extended, structured answers with grammatical range. At Band 5.5 the problem is usually answer length and grammar variety, not pronunciation.

SpeakingADD FormulaIDEA StructureGrammar Range

🎯 Part 1 — The ADD Formula

Never give a one-sentence answer. Every Part 1 response should have three components:

A — Answer the question directly (1 sentence) D — Develop it: explain or describe (1–2 sentences) D — Detail: give a specific example, time, or reason
✗ Band 5 — Too short
"Yes, I like cooking."
✓ Band 7 — ADD formula applied
"I genuinely enjoy cooking, especially at weekends when I have more time. I find it quite therapeutic — there's something satisfying about preparing a meal from scratch. Last Sunday I tried making a Thai green curry for the first time, and it actually turned out really well."

🎤 Part 2 — The IDEA Structure (2 minutes)

I — Introduce the topic (1–2 sentences) D — Describe the main details (who, what, where, when) E — Explain why it is significant / how it made you feel A — Attitude: your personal reflection or opinion about it

Time-buying phrases (not fillers):

  • That's an interesting question to consider…
  • Let me think about that for a moment…
  • There are several things I could mention here…
  • What comes to mind first is…

🧠 Grammar Range — What Band 7 Looks Like

StructureBand 7 Example
Past perfectBy the time I arrived, they had already started.
ConditionalsIf I had more time, I would travel more often.
Passive voiceThe building was constructed in the 1980s.

Wish / regretI wish I had studied harder when I was younger.
Gerund subjectLearning a new language requires enormous dedication.
Processing Instruction — Before recording

Write one sentence using EACH grammar structure above about your Part 2 topic. Then use at least two of them in your spoken answer. This is pushed output (Swain, 1985) — it forces you to use structures you would normally avoid.

💬 Part 3 — Discussion Answer Structure

Position → State your view directly Reason → Explain why (one developed reason) Example → Give a real or plausible example Counter → Acknowledge the other side briefly Return → Come back to your original position
✓ Band 7 Part 3 Answer
"On balance, I'd say the effects of social media are largely negative, particularly for younger generations. The constant exposure to curated images of other people's lives tends to fuel anxiety and lower self-esteem. In countries like South Korea, for instance, there has been a notable rise in depression rates that researchers have linked to social media use. That said, it would be unfair to ignore the genuine benefits — platforms like Twitter have given marginalised communities a voice they previously lacked. Nevertheless, the harms seem to outweigh the advantages for most users."

Day 5 · Friday

Writing Task 1 — Academic Data Essay

The Overview paragraph is the single feature that most clearly separates Band 6 from Band 7. Most 5.5 students do not write one at all.

Writing Task 1Overview paragraphData language20 min task

❌ Why Band 5.5 Students Struggle with Task 1

Four Core Errors
  • They describe every single data point instead of selecting and grouping
  • They do not write a clear Overview paragraph (biggest Band 7 requirement)
  • They copy numbers without any language variety
  • They describe what the chart is rather than what it shows

📐 The Band 7 Task 1 Formula

¶ 1 — Introduction (2 sentences) → Paraphrase: "The chart/graph/diagram illustrates…" → Do NOT copy the rubric word for word ¶ 2 — Overview (2–3 sentences) ← THIS IS THE KEY → State 2–3 MAJOR TRENDS without using specific numbers → This is what separates Band 6 from Band 7 ¶ 3 — Key Feature 1 (3–4 sentences) → Describe with specific data + comparison language ¶ 4 — Key Feature 2 (3–4 sentences) → Describe with specific data, contrast or elaborate
The Overview — What it actually is

The overview answers: "What is the most important thing this chart shows?" It is a bird's-eye view with no numbers.

✗ Band 5 "Overview"
"There are many different types of energy shown in the chart."
✓ Band 7 Overview
"Overall, it is clear that renewable energy sources experienced a significant increase over the period, while fossil fuel consumption declined steadily, with solar power recording the most dramatic growth."

Day 7 · Sunday

Immersion + Rest Protocol

Vocabulary acquisition and grammar internalisation occur during sleep and low-intensity exposure. Cramming on Sunday causes burnout. This day is deliberately light.

ImmersionPassive inputAnki review onlyReflection

🧪 Why Rest is Evidence-Based

Dynamic Systems Theory (Larsen-Freeman, 1997) models language acquisition as non-linear — gains are consolidated during low-activity periods, not only during intensive study. Pushing 8 hours of study on Sunday does not add to Monday's performance; it depletes the cognitive resources that would otherwise consolidate Friday and Saturday's gains.

📚 The Input Method for New Vocabulary

When you encounter a new word today (Nation's 4-Strand Model — Depth of Processing):

  1. Note the context — what was the article or talk about?
  2. Find 2–3 collocations in a dictionary (not just the definition)
  3. Write one sentence using it in a context you care about
  4. Add to Anki tonight — collocations on the back, not just meaning
